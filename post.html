Medical Research Is At Risk

Why We Must Speak Up

Posted by Andrew Richmond on April 29, 2025

In the face of budget cuts, political agendas, and administrative overreach, the United States is halting some of its medical and scientific research programs. The defunding and pausing of clinical trials, university grants, and scientific publications is a direct threat to human lives, public health, and our nation’s future.

As someone with deep personal ties to the medical world, I cannot watch this happen in silence. My mother and aunt are nurses, dedicated to healing. My father works in health insurance, and I am preparing to enter this field as well. But above all, I’m speaking out because my uncle has ALS, a relentless, degenerative disease that robs people of their mobility, independence, and, eventually, their lives.

Recently, I learned that the clinical trials for ALS treatments may be halted due to sweeping budget cuts and federal restrictions. According to the ALS Association, “That funding is essential. People living with ALS have no time to waste.” That statement echoes for millions of people across the country with rare, deadly, or poorly understood illnesses. The clock is always ticking not just for those with ALS, but for those battling cancer, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, HIV, and many more. Research delays don’t just slow progress, they cost lives.

Yet instead of accelerating our search for treatments, we are pulling back.

A Political Agenda at the Cost of Progress

In February, Reuters reported that the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) was ordered to withdraw all pending scientific papers involving its researchers. This move was tied to President Trump’s executive orders redefining the federal government’s stance on gender and diversity, effectively censoring research that references LGBTQ+ health issues. Leading journal editors have raised constitutional concerns, citing violations of the First Amendment and academic freedom.

This order undermines years of ongoing work and sets a dangerous precedent that politics can dictate science. When a paper can be shelved because it uses the “wrong” words, scientific integrity is no longer leading public health policy, ideology is.

And it’s not just publications. The Trump administration has pulled federal funding from major research institutions, including Columbia University and Johns Hopkins to enforce political compliance. These universities rely heavily on federal research dollars and in some cases, it accounts for nearly half of their annual revenue. When those funds vanish, so do thousands of jobs, countless research projects, and the hope they provide for patients.

Johns Hopkins, for instance, recently announced 2,200 layoffs following grant reductions. These cuts affect not just defense or engineering projects but medical fields where progress has life-or-death consequences.

This Is Not a Budget Issue. It’s a Values Issue

These cuts have been framed as fiscal responsibility or administrative streamlining. But there is nothing efficient about canceling a trial that could lead to life-saving treatment. There is nothing conservative about eliminating studies on youth mental health or global HIV prevention. These are political statements disguised as policy statements that declare some people and some lives less worthy of support.

Medical research takes years, often decades. A pause today means a cure might come too late for an entire generation. Diseases do not wait for political cycles to end. Funding delays do not freeze disease progression in real life. For patients with ALS and countless others, time is not a luxury.

What Can We Do?

We can’t afford to wait quietly.

We must raise awareness about what’s at stake. Viral movements like the Ice Bucket Challenge reminded the world that visibility can turn into action and action into funding. Public consciousness matters.

Contact your representatives. Demand clarity and accountability. Ask them why life-saving research is being used as a political pawn. Urge them to restore funding and protect the autonomy of our research institutions.

Medical research is not optional. It is the very foundation of public health, medical advancement, and human dignity. We must fight to protect it.
